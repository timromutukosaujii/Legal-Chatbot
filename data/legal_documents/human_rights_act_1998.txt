Title: Human Rights Act 1998 (Plain-Language Overview)
Source: UK Public General Acts - Human Rights Act 1998
URL: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1998/42/contents
Topic: UK Constitutional and Human Rights Law

This document is a plain-language summary for educational use.
It explains core ideas from the Human Rights Act 1998 and is not a substitute for legal advice.

The Human Rights Act 1998 (HRA) brings most rights from the European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR)
into UK law. This means people can rely on these rights in UK courts without always going to the European
Court of Human Rights in Strasbourg.

Core points:
- Public authorities (for example, councils, police, NHS bodies, and many government departments) must act
  in a way that is compatible with Convention rights.
- UK courts should interpret legislation, so far as possible, in a way compatible with Convention rights.
- If a higher UK court finds that an Act of Parliament cannot be interpreted compatibly, it may issue a
  declaration of incompatibility.
- A declaration does not automatically cancel the law, but signals Parliament to consider change.

Examples of protected rights often discussed in UK legal practice:
- Article 2: right to life
- Article 3: prohibition of torture and inhuman or degrading treatment
- Article 5: right to liberty and security
- Article 6: right to a fair trial
- Article 8: right to respect for private and family life
- Article 9: freedom of thought, conscience and religion
- Article 10: freedom of expression
- Article 11: freedom of assembly and association
- Article 14: prohibition of discrimination in relation to Convention rights

Limits and balancing:
Some rights are absolute (for example Article 3), while others are qualified (for example Article 8 and
Article 10). Qualified rights may be limited when the law allows it and when a limit is necessary and
proportionate for a legitimate aim, such as public safety or protecting others' rights.

In practice:
The HRA is often used in judicial review and public law cases. It also influences day-to-day decisions by
public bodies, encouraging rights-based decision-making and accountability.
